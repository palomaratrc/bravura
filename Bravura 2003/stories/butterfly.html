The Butterfly Man

     Two old friends sat together sharing what little shade they could find on a hot July afternoon. Not much conversation passed between the two men; they were content to let the sun-crazed buzzing of the junebugs fill in the silence. Afternoons here on Cleveland Kohl’s porch were usually passed in just this fashion and both men, Cleveland and Orville Pickins, liked it that way just fine. So, you can imagine Orville’s surprise to hear his old friend suddenly break with tradition and say, “Wulllllp, there he goes again.”
     Orville stopped chewin’ on his toothpick. “What’s that, Cleve?”
     “That fool Butterfly Man over there, Orville.” He pointed across the porch with a gnarled finger. “He’s right there over in McFarland’s cow patch, ya dang fool, can’t you see?”
     Orville squinted up his face and nodded, looking for the life of him like a chihuahua in overalls. “Of course, I see him now, Cleveland.” he lied. “Just had the sun in my eyes is all.” He knew his eyes were getting bad but he didn’t want some old fart like Cleve knowing all about it. It was bad enough his old woman was always on his back to go and see some eye doctor. He squinched up his face tighter and damned his old eyes. “I bet old man McFarland’s gonna raise some hell when he sees that fella over there on his land. Gonna be some shootin’, I bet.”
     Cleveland sat back, his hands over his belly and rocked his chair slowly. “Naw, there won’t.”
     Orville looked over towards his friend; he could see it coming. Cleveland sitting there, rocking with his hands held tight across his belly. That’s how you could really tell: Cleve’s hands. If he kept them open to his sides, there was nothing to worry about, just friendly conversation and shooting the breeze. If he started wiping his face as though he was sweating when there wasn’t a sun in the sky, that spelled an argument brewing. But when he kept them tight and close to his chest, like he was holding a winning poker hand, then Orville knew there was a story coming up. So, Orville smiled and played his part. “Now how the hell would you know that?”
     Cleve looked over at Orville and spit a dark stream off the porch. “Ain’t nothing new. I’ve seen him once or twice before over in that field. He’s butterfly huntin’, see? That’s why I call him the Butterfly Man.”
     “If he’s so common how come I ain’t never seen him before?”
     “Guess you weren’t lookin’ before. I wouldn’t’ve noticed him myself if it weren’t for my old lady Mae showing him out to me. She saw him once or twice a summer before she passed on, God love her.”
     Cleve closed his eyes. Orville squinted up his little Chihuahua face and looked on towards old man McFarland’s field. The sun was at about 3’oclock so it wasn’t anywhere in his eyes but he’d be hanged if he could see a damn thing over in that field but overgrown weeds and a dying old tree.
     There weren’t no Butterfly Man nowhere.
     “Yep, he’s a sight, ain’t he?”
     Orville jumped at the voice. “Yep…..that he sure is.”
     “I figure he must pay McFarland a pretty penny to go jumpin’ around in his fields. I’ve never heard him complaining about it. Lord knows he grips about everything else.”
     Orville chuckled deep in his chest. “McFarland is a rascal, tight with his money., that’s for sure. But what do you suppose that man does with all those butterflies? Think he’s one of them people that kills ‘em and hangs ‘em on the wall?”
     “Don’t know about that. Never had the mind to go up and ask him. In my opinion, I have to agree with Mae. She used to say it was cruel what he was doing out there. ‘Butterflies are like souls’, she used to say, ‘they aren’t something to be netted and collected into jars.’ The kinds of people that would go and do something like that is sick.”
     “How’s that?”
     “Well, just take a look at a butterfly. They don’t harm nothin’, don’t get anybody’s way. Why kill them? My Mae always loved butterflies. I remember how she’d embroider them on those fancy pillows that she sold at the flea market. Remember them, Orville? They were always so pretty.”
     “Yessir, Cleveland, you married yourself a fine woman. A fine woman.”
     “And wasn’t she a sight? All her life. Never did stop shining.”
     “As pretty as a picture, if I can say so. Yessir, she was that.”
     Cleveland looked down at his hands and stopped talking. Orville stayed quiet and turned his face away from his friend. Some things were just meant to be done in private.
     Cleveland pulled a handkerchief out of his pocket and blew his nose loudly. “I’m getting’ too damn old when I start crying like a baby over nothin’.”
     “We’re never too old to remember, Cleve, and, well…sometimes that’s enough.”
     “I guess that’s the gospel truth. This happens to me every time I see him over there jumpin’ and wavin’ that fool net of his around like it was Old Glory. I watch him for a spell and then I start to remembering and then I…oh, hell.” He pushed himself up from his rocking chair. “Don’t go on listenin’ to me make a dang fool outta myself, Orville. Come on inside and let’s get us a beer or two. It’s starting to get too hot out here anyway.”
     “Naw, Cleveland, thank you kindly but it’s getting late. I’d best be getting on towards home if I want to get a few chores done before suppertime.” Orville stood up and lightly fingered the oil stained bill of his cap. “Why don’t you come on over and have supper with the old lady and me? She’d be sore with me if I didn’t ask you.”
     Cleveland’s face dropped slightly. “No, thank you and the missus but, no thanks. I’m feeling a might tired. I think I’ll just go lie down a spell until I feel better.”
     Orville eyed his friend. “See ya tomorrow?”
     “Tomorrow.” Cleveland said and waved his friend on.
     After Orville had gone far enough down the lane to be out of sight, Cleveland went inside, grabbed a beer and sat back down in his rocking chair. Cleveland rocked slowly, back and forth, back and forth. A solid white cloud shrouded the sun and cast a shadow over his face as his head fell to his chest. The bottle he held in his hand fell heavily and rolled across the porch. A hoot owl hooted in the distance and a breeze blew through his cotton white hair. The figure in the field continued on with his St. Vitus dance amid a circling whirlwind of colors. The mesh of his net billowed out like a cloud as he reached towards a passing butterfly.
