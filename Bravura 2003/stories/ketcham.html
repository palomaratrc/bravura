Last Summer in Ketcham

For me, the summer of 1959 always begins with the single engine Cessna circling the little airstrip near Uncle Ernie’s home in Ketcham, Idaho. Every other summer, as we approached, a tiny black speck on the green patch of grass near the runway would materialize into one of the hired hands.

Failing to disguise my disappointment, my mother would try to smooth things over by reminding me that Uncle Ernie was very busy writing and would have been here if he could have. But that summer, I saw the speck grow into a familiar big shouldered man wearing a broad brimmed hat. Uncle Ernie.

For fourteen summers mother and I went to his home on the lake. The trips started the year I was born, the same year my dad died. Mother said it was important that I get to know my father’s only brother. The two brothers walked in each other’s shadow when they were boys, she explained. She told me when I was born, each reveled in the prospect of my new life and planned to include me in their adventures. When a German fighter plane forced my father’s aircraft deep into a snow covered field in 1944 their plans inextricably changed. Staying close to Uncle Ernie was mother’s way of keeping my father alive, but I only got to see him once a year because our home in New York was too far away for more trips and mother’s busy editing job at Scribners didn’t allow for long vacations. I knew the annual trip was coming, so I was content. It was worth the wait.

Preparations for our summer trip never varied. Right after Christmas, mother would begin to talk about visiting Uncle Ernie. She’d pull out our photo album and we’d reminisce, looking at snap shots of carefree summers past while Manhattan snow flurries unwrapped around our apartment. Mother’s eyes glistened as she studied the dogeared photographs of herself hugging a tall man whose face resembled Uncle Ernie’s. She’d hold me closer and closer as she turned the pages, retelling the events the photos had captured.

"Here’s your father and Ernie trying to stand in a canoe," she laughed aloud."This one was taken at the end of the pier. Look at the tiny fish they caught!"

The delicate lines around her eyes would relax and her soft lips turn up in tender smiles as she spoke of my father and Uncle Ernie, his big brother. Her gaze would linger on my father’s grinning face captured by the click of a shutter long ago. She must have missed him and the life they were supposed to live with me. At the time, I never considered our lives would be any different, because mother was devoted to me; afraid of losing the only link she had to my father’s love. Her caresses were constant reminders that still leave vivid impressions: a stroke of my hair, a kiss on my forehead, her hands cupping my face as she spoke softly to me.

By April I’d start sorting through piles of keepsakes from the year to share with Uncle Ernie anticipating the trip ahead. I looked forward to the way he picked up a school drawing or a math paper with its imperfect score or my newest baseball cards, and then examine them as if each was a rare document. His questions would come at me fast: What were you thinking about when you drew this? How did you come to that conclusion? Why is this player batting last? He’d stare at me the way a hawk watches its quarry, reacting to my answers with a puzzled frown, one raised eyebrow or his index finger stroking his beard. Because my responses were often ideas made up on the spot, he seemed pleased. Uncle Ernie didn’t mind that for a girl, I liked baseball more than dolls, and fishing more than playing dress up. I think he saw a bit of my father in me. He noticed traits I wouldn’t recognize until I was a grown woman missing him and the father he had replaced.

That summer, after we landed and unfolded ourselves from the Cessna, Uncle Ernie and my mother rode in the cab of his cherry red pickup truck while I shared the open back with the suitcases and his dog, Bigger, a black Labrador retriever who always remembered me with wet kisses. I leaned back against the tailgate, held Bigger to my chest, and let the wind blow New York from my lungs as we bumped up the highway.

At the end of the dusty driveway that stretched over a mile from the main road, his low slung, wood house settled into a grove of pine trees facing the lake. He’d lived in the rambling warren of mismatched rooms for as long as I could remember. Over the years he had filled it with his books, mementos, wives, and children, all now gone. Big game hunting trophies crowded the walls. The chairs were worn and easy to slip into, like comfortable old shoes, and if sand sifted onto the rugs scattered throughout, no one cared. From the front of the house, a wide green lawn sloped to the lakeshore from which a long pier jutted out on watery legs. It was always the same. Nothing new, nothing changed. A perfect summer about to begin.

Uncle Ernie nosed the pickup into the barn, scattering the few chickens that lived there, slammed the driver’s door, then lifted me high above his head against the sun. Looking down, my laughing face was reflected in his mirrored sunglasses. I couldn’t see his warm brown eyes, but I knew they were wrinkled up in delight. At fourteen I was small, yet still too heavy for this ritual. The strain showed on his face, but I’m sure my giggles made up for the effort.

As he walked us to the house, his arm around my shoulder felt reassuring, and I remember feeling that nothing could change our time together.

Routine settled in as it always did. At dawn, he’d poke his head in my room and hurry me to pull on my clothes and meet him at the end of the dock. Mist shrouded the lake, curling around us as we stood like two ghosts flicking our fishing poles. With a slithering ping, our lines sliced through the moist air until the hook with its metallic disk fluttered onto the lake surface. We didn’t talk. Uncle Ernie said voices scared the fish. We just grunted a happy little sound when the lure dropped in a particularly nice spot. As the sun warmed up the July morning, it drew musky scents of crayfish and decaying leaves from the reedy shore and pulled the mists back into the lake revealing our figures: Uncle Ernie, intent on his arcing line, while I hoped my feeble attempts to mimic his perfect casts didn’t fail.

To fail him would be inexcusable, and I tried hard to live up to his expectations. His presence was larger than anything else in my life. He’d faced bulls in Spain, fought in wars, safaried in Africa, and reaped awards for the stories of his exploits.

The rest of the day, after the sun began to burn through the back of our shirts and the fish had dropped down into the cool bottom of the lake, Uncle Ernie worked. He wasn’t to be bothered then, my mother told me. But sometimes I’d sneak up to the open door of his office and sit cross-legged on the floor watching him. He’d write standing up to relieve the pain in his back, his head bent and his broad shoulders hunched over the tall desk.

That summer the work was different. Big hands and meaty fingers that had curled delicately around a fishing rod earlier, now hammered furiously on the keys of his battered Corona Number 4. Eyes fixed on the crisp paper rolled into the typewriter, he was lost in the rat-a-tat-tat of keys hitting paper in furious volleys. The cadence that once sounded melodic to me was now interrupted by the shrill whine of paper ripped from the typewriter. Balled up then thrown accompanied by eruptions of profanity, the crumpled paper rested on book shelves and spilled out of his waste basket. His usually tidy office was strewn with messy stacks of pages bearing red slashes through the words.

Every so often, he would inhale mightily, sucking in fresh air from the open window facing his desk. He’d fill his lungs as if the thoughts and stories he wrote were drifting in on the cool currents.

By six o’clock, Uncle Ernie would stop, snap the worn cover atop the Corona, and light a cigar. That was my signal to regain contact. He’d pour an inch or so from a bottle of Havana Club into a squat glass, grab the bottle, then beckon me from the hallway. I’d follow him outside to sit on the porch swing and together we would watch the sunset over the lake.

"Carrie Ann, do you remember your father?" He often asked this question. Sometimes with intensity, sometimes nonchalantly like he didn’t really want to know, but just to remind me that I did have a father once. I glanced at his bearded silhouette, mesmerized by the rings of cigar smoke hanging in the air above his head.

"Not much," I answered, hoping for new stories about my father.

"Brave man. Flew planes over Germany in WWII. Died there." Walking to the edge of the porch, he peered at the lake, his hand sheltering his eyes from the sun’s rays glinting on its surface. The white shirt that stretched across his broad back glowed in the dwindling light, and the briny scent of his work day hung in the air. He gazed at the horizon, his head slowly moving from side to side. When he turned back to face me his eyes were damp.

"We both went. I came back, he didn’t. Brave. Yes, he was." His voice trailed off into the dregs of the Havana Club.

I’d heard the war story before. "Mother said he killed people," I said softly.

"He didn’t want to. Had to because he was a soldier. They kill because they have to. Soldiers are brave." His whispered words took on a hollow dejected tone like when you’ve been disappointed and can’t do anything about it.

"You killed animals. Did you have to do that, too?" I asked, thinking of the mounted heads of elk, big horn sheep and bear scattered throughout the house.

"No, it’s different. I hunted animals for sport. The killing is unimportant. I just did it because..." Uncle Ernie’s face sagged. "Not brave, you know." He wiped the sleeve of his shirt across his eyes.

Night had captured the sky in layers of purple and gray. Pale stars were emerging above the watery blue haze hovering on the lake. The screen door creaked behind me and I sat alone watching the darkening Idaho sky come alive.

"Come inside, kiddo." Yellow lamplight spilled from the doorway framing Uncle Ernie’s big body and glinting through the amber liquid in the glass he held.

The gleaming row of guns carefully locked inside the tall glass-fronted cabinet by the front door were off limits to me. Their foreboding presence drew me to them anyway. As I passed them, I always stopped to study them.

"They’re Uncle Ernie’s," mother reminded me, "No one but him may touch them."

He patiently wrote their names down on a scrap of paper when I was ten. I can still recite their names from memory: a 416 blue-black Perugini side-by-side rifle, a glossy 30/06 Ruger with an engraved barrel, a 7mm Weatherby, its carved stock a grainy black and brown, a 405 Remington like the one Teddy Roosevelt used, a 308 Browning with a deer head engraved near the trigger, and a stubby double-barreled Boss shotgun.

The guns fascinated me, just like the blank glass eyes of the animal heads mounted on the walls and the huge brown bear in a corner standing on its back feet, mouth open in rage. The guns were real. Not like the headless dead animals whose bodies were gone and forgotten.

"Carrie Ann, come away from the gun case," Mother’s soft voice instructed. I looked over my shoulder at her, but I didn’t move. My mother kept a worried eye on me.

The furrows between her eyebrows deepened and her mouth straightened into a tense line, as they did whenever I revealed a part of me she didn’t understand.

"Let her be." Uncle Ernie had walked into the room.

His hand rested on my shoulder, and Mother backed away wordless.

"You see the big one?" He gently pushed me towards the Perugini rifle. "That one is for elephants."

The glass felt cool to my fingertips where I timidly touched it above the rifle, then furtively glancing at mother, quickly dropped my hand to my side.

His knuckles tapped across the glass, stopping at each gun displayed in the lethal row I’d memorized.

"Tiger...bear...rhino...gazelle..." his deep voice grew quieter with every word.

"Each one has its own purpose. Do you understand?" His wide sweeping gesture took in the walls around us. He stared down at me, eyes narrowed into slits.

I nodded, but I didn’t understand. Then.

The Boss shotgun rested in last place in the case. He looked at it for a moment.

"I haven’t used that one yet," he said, pausing."It’s all you’d need to kill a man." He glanced at the gun again then turned away, disappearing into the hallway. Within moments, the sound of ice clinked into a glass and the smell of cigar wafted from the recesses of his office as the door closed softly behind him.

The summer weeks unfolded and our routine varied a bit; some days we took a trip into town. Just Uncle Ernie, Bigger and me. Everyone knew us. The postmaster

would slip Bigger a cookie along with Uncle Ernie’s mail and packages and always, a stick of Black Jack gum for me. We’d stop for a fresh bottle of Havana Club at Willie’s Liquors & Spirits, where Uncle Ernie would pick up an armload of newspapers from far away places, and finally the Piggly Wiggly to fill up our cart from mother’s list.

We’d made those stops today.

"What’s in the mail?" Uncle Ernie glanced over at me.

I had the bulky pile on my lap and was inspecting the intriguing colorful stamps as we drove home.

"Three envelopes from New York, one from Paris, and the package is from Los Angeles." At the bottom of the pile, I balanced a flat brown paper wrapped box on my knees.

"Open the box, and tell me what’s on top." His eyes were on the road, but the sound in his voice made my fingers hurry as I tore open the wrapping and lifted the lid. A letter lay on top of a thick sheaf of papers.

"Read the letter to me." His voice rasped, a dry tight sound.

"Dear Mr. He...." I began to read.

"Skip that part, just read the message," he interrupted.

"This is your best work, old pal. It looks like you’ve got a winner here. It’s bursting with passion and bold intrigue. One publisher is so eager to read it I’m meeting

him tomorrow to..."

"Passion? By God, ‘passion’! What does that SOB know about passion?" He pounded the dashboard with his fist. "I know what he’s doing...bullshit! He knows the passion’s gone. The book’s no good. By God, he feels sorry for me. Damned SOB!" His big fist hit the dashboard again.

I jumped as Bigger struggled from my grasp, barking at the sudden sound.

"But isn’t this good?" I didn’t understand. The letter praised his work.

"No! No. No good. I’ve failed and he knows it. Patronizing son of a bitch."

The veins on Uncle Ernie’s neck stood out as he gripped the steering wheel tighter.The knuckle bones of his big hands turned yellow-white. His foot pushed the accelerator down to the floor sending the pickup truck lurching along the road. Holding on to Bigger’s collar, I braced my feet against the floorboard and jounced up and down on the seat. Bigger collapsed against me. As the full weight of the big dog pinned me against the door, I grabbed the armrest to keep my balance. The pile of mail slid to the floor.

Uncle Ernie’s face reddened as his brown eyes grew black and widened. I could taste the dust and exhaust billowing behind the pickup truck as it blew into the open window.

"Stop! Stop!" I shouted, pounding his shoulder with my balled up fists. For the first time in my life I was afraid of him. Afraid of what might happen. It was as if he didn’t remember Bigger and I were there, or even where he was.

"Stop, stop, please stop. I’m afraid!", I yelled again, glimpsing the blur of trees along side the road as the truck sped faster.

Then as quickly as his black rage emerged in all its fury, it stopped.

He eased back on the gas pedal and the truck began to slow as he turned it onto the shoulder of the road in a flurry of crunching gravel, then cut the engine.

Afraid to move, I sat motionless. A warm trickle of sweat ran down my spine and my gulped breath, coming in short painful bursts, began to subside.

Running his hands through his hair, he stared blankly through the window, then turned to me, shoulders slumped. His tanned face looked sallow beneath his beard and tears filled his eyes.

He cupped my chin in his hand and scanned my face taking in my wild blond hair, the freckles on my nose, and my brown wide-set eyes like his. I stared into his drawn face and felt something break between us forever. I couldn't look away.

"Ahh, kiddo," he moaned from deep inside his chest. "I didn’t mean to scare you. Don’t be afraid. Don’t be afraid to fail. Not like me. Be brave like your father."

"You haven’t failed." I searched his face, wanting to say the right thing, to comfort him.

"I have failed. The work isn’t good anymore. I know it. Time to be brave and... I don’t think I can do it." His deep voice wavered and he looked away. Thinking back, looking at me must have reminded him of a tomorrow he no longer could see.

"You haven’t failed me," I mumbled, hoping my loyalty and love would be enough.

No matter what I said, I believe he knew something inside him was dying.

His hand dropped from my face as he turned back to stare at the road ahead, the fields and mountains beyond it and to the horizon where an unknown future lay.

I flung my arms around his neck, burying my face in the softness of his beard. Cars whizzed by. We sat there alone, unaware of time until shadows deepening in the corners of the cab veiled our faces.

He spoke at last. "Better get home. Your mother will be worried."

The string had broken on the sheaf of papers and they laid in disarray on the floorboard. I started to gather them up.

"Never mind those, " he said. "They’re nothing to me now."

We rode home not speaking, the silence broken only by Bigger’s panting.

Summer was drawing to a close. In Idaho, fall nips at August mornings, urging shoes onto bare feet and sweat shirts over jeans. Reluctantly, I began sorting my collection of curious stones, pressed flowers, and feathers, choosing what to keep and what to leave behind, and thinking about the trip home. Soon it would be time to return to New York. I wondered if summers would ever be the same again.

Uncle Ernie hadn’t worked since the day I’d opened the box from Los Angeles and read the letter to him. He still went to his office after fishing, but now he either read in front of the open window or stared at the landscape. The keys of the Corona remained untouched and Willie’s Liquors & Spirits delivered Havana Club to the house by the case. As the days shortened, Bigger obediently padded after me, his nails clicking on the wood floors echoing through the quiet house, but he sought Uncle Ernie’s companionship more and more.

The day we left, I looked down at Uncle Ernie’s diminishing shape as the Cessna rose higher into the sky. His beard jutted upwards as he pulled his hat from his head and waved it slowly in the air. In the distance, the sun was setting. Layers of cool purple and gray mist would again settle onto the lake, and if I closed my eyes I could smell Havana Club and the sweet scent of cigar.

All these years later, I know why I was there that summer. To say goodbye.
