CHOCOLATE

by Cyndy Martin

 

                My grandfather had a special place in his house.  It was his big leather recliner with a side table.  Anything on that table was his and only his.  The top shelf was not interesting at all.  There was a glass ash tray overflowing with butts and ashes and usually a newspaper folded in half lengthwise and tossed haphazardly against a cut crystal lamp.  But the lower shelf had the treasure.  This was hard to take for a young child: he kept at least one of those huge one pound Hershey’s chocolate bars.  I don’t remember being told they were his, but I was so sure about that fact that I never even thought about asking for any.  My grandmother made up for this by having a candy dish full of goodies we could have.  It was a system that kept everyone happy, more or less.

                My mother kept up the forbidden Hershey treasure tradition.  She had the smaller bars, the ones that had H-E-R-S-H-E-Y spelled out on the surface of the chocolate.  Her six packs were hidden behind the dinner dishes in the cupboard above the kitchen counter.  I knew her spot and I learned I could sneak one candy bar, just so long as it wasn’t the last one, and she never seemed to notice.  By age eight I was very good at vaulting up onto the counter, landing on my knees, and getting behind those dishes. 

                Unfortunately, I have not been able to continue the tradition.  I had one of those odd pregnancy changes.  Some women get curly hair where it had always been straight, others grow stronger nails, some just get stretch marks.  I developed an allergic reaction to chocolate.  Ever since my daughter was born, I get migraine headaches from chocolate.  But I have strong memories.

                I am sad to say that my daughter didn’t get to meet the grandfather I grew up with.  Oh, she met him, but he was no longer himself.  He developed Alzheimer’s by the time she was a year old.  When she was three, his wild gray hair and eyebrows made him look like a scary Albert Einstein.  One Christmas day at my mother’s house, my daughter and her cousin held hands and walked up to him and bravely shouted in unison, “You can’t scare us!  It’s not OK!”  Then they ran upstairs to the playroom as fast as they could.  My mom stopped bringing him over on Christmas after that.  She was afraid the kids would be traumatized.  I couldn’t understand that.  I still saw my funny grandpa, just more worn out and understandably annoyed by the squealing little kids.  I still loved him being with us.  I had all the patience in the world for the fishing story he would repeat ten times in one afternoon.

                A few years later, I had a very vivid dream.  I dreamt that my grandfather had come to visit me at my new house.  He had never really been there before.  In the dream, we talked like we did long ago sitting on a worn wooden bench in his long narrow garden with a goldfish pond in the center of the lawn and beautiful blooms weighing heavily on shrubs all around the perimeter of the yard in San Francisco.  Not one bit of his story telling was repetitious.  He pretended I was a little girl again and he clicked his nails in my ear like his used to do, saying, “It’s little buggies in your ear.  Hear them?”  I laughed and held his big, rough hand covered with age spots and wiry hair as I pulled him away from my ear.  Then he gave me part of his one pound Hershey bar.  I felt like he had just given me a diamond necklace.  I gasped quickly in, and slowly out, as I felt the heavy weight of the honor sitting high on my chest.  We both ate the chocolate and smiled great, big happy smiles.  And I felt warm.

                I woke up in the morning with a migraine.  It was worse than usual.  I asked my husband to bring me my pills and some water, then I told him about the dream.  I was pressing my left temple while I chuckled.  I stayed in bed for a couple of hours, waiting for the pain to subside. 

Just as I was ready to get up, the phone rang.  It was my mother.  My grandfather had died in the night.  Peacefully, on the leather recliner in front of the TV.  I imagine they found him smiling on the chair with a big Hershey wrapper on his lap.  And I was grateful that he had come to say goodbye to me.
