The Collector
Sarah Bates
     "Cans," he says, clearly talking to me, "I work in cans."
     "What?" I say, turning to face him. I'm walking out Safeway's door with two shopping bags heavy with six packs of Diet Pepsi in my hands.
     Long hair pulled back with a rubber band reveals a gaunt face, a sharp beak of a nose scabby with peeling skin, wispy tendrils of a beard. Clothes the color of the earth he walks and a worn khaki backpack on his narrow frame distinguish him. Long fingers grip a bulging Hefty bag flung over one bony shoulder. His presence fills the air around us; I feel a vacuum snap shut.
     "I work in cans. Collect 'em for the world," he says, rolling the bag off his shoulder to the ground in a rattle of clicks and clanks. It rests against his leg, thigh high.
     I've spotted him striding up East Vista Way near my house, as if the burdens he carries are weightless. Up close he looks different than I expect.
     Thrusting a hand into the back pocket of his chinos, he pulls out a business card and holds it out. His nails are trimmed and clean. "See? Cans."
     I pinch the card gingerly between my thumb and forefinger. It's wrinkled and rimmed with dirt as if it's passed through many hands. "World On My Back Can Collecting, A. Luminum, Owner," I read.
     The man peers at me waiting for acknowledgement. His blue eyes are clear and serene under graying bushy eyebrows.
     "You're 'A. Luminum'?" I ask, suppressing a laugh. The earnest, open expression on his face telegraphs caution as I hand back the card. Dense, fruity air swirls toward me drawing the vacuum tighter.
     "Didn't used to be, but I changed it when I figured out how important the name was. People pay money for it, you know, so I changed it." He smiles revealing a chipped front tooth then sees me glance at his mouth and quickly raises his hand to cover it.
     "Tried to bite off a can lid," he says from behind his hand, low and muffled.
     "Does the 'A' stand for something; an abbreviation...?" My curiosity is like a magnet. The fruit smell stuffs my nose, thickening effervescent, choking in my throat. I can't pull away from this bearded soul whose frequent appearance so often caught my attention. "Just 'A'," he says. "Makes it easy for people calling my name, like you know, 'A, you want these cans?' " He glances down at the bags of six-packs in my hands.
     "These aren't empty yet," I say, gulping for breath. Backing away from him at last, I hear a soft hiss.
     "Uh, that's okay, too heavy anyhow. I like 'em light." He grins wide, the chipped tooth a gaping hole.
     I nod, then turn toward my car, take a few steps and look back. When I do, I swear I can see the faint image of the Safeway sign reflecting off his torso.
     "Don't forget me when they're empty," he calls out, waving his business card. "A. Luminum."
