Counting Castles in the Sand
Diane Sawyer

For too long
I’ve written you up and down,
poured oceans into rivers, half a life
distilled into little-ease,
ink tincturing a scent, a single brow
propriety would never let me touch -
even modulations of your mimicry,
for every day we circled round the Isles of Shoales
to tack for home, sheets harnessed to a setting sun,
for one small bottle, fragile message sealed within,
slipped between the pages of the sea,
coming years too late.
Too long
I’ve written you up and down
this mounting stay of obituary, stringing
saturated strands of sentences,
curing in your absence
where the bight of Long Sands
cups the sea, an open wound of who we were;
a siblings’ syntax canting castles
in the sand, for every tender token sifted
from your hand to mine,
and washed away.
Too long
I’ve written you up and down
where even gull and godwit etched
their own patois of you, the Kaddish
stitched across my tongue,
bound by strictures of the littoral,
combing darker tides of grief, every low tide
bleeding through the cracks,
each high tide choking me in words of you,
until heart, distended, turned to haggis.
But now, the words of loss have all been spent,
the spinnaker, lifts and fills, the younger,
finally an elder reaching
for a fuller lifetime. Your words,
God’s, or mine, they have no up,
no down, no right -
not even wrong; instead, each can only drop behind,
brilliant colors on a breathing sea, markers
should I ever wish to return to you,
whether for just an hour, or for a day.
