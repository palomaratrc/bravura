“Advice to Poets”
Adam Marley


If you want to be a poet
it is really quite
simple.
First, you must take out your eyes
and screw them in backwards.
Watch the creative impulses
as they bounce around in your brain,
vivacious chemicals screaming.
Second, you must shove your head
between two fluffy pillows
until all outside sound is drowned out
and all you hear is your heartbeat,
for that is the rhythm you will use.
Third, you must peel off your flesh
and run about through a field of grass
so that you may
feel the blades as they grasp
at your sticky insides.
Fourth, you must swallow your tongue
and have it dance
through the deep pits
of your body.
Have it taste your blood,
your bile,
your soul.
If through all this
you finally find
your creative center
and if there you see
a little cantankerous gnome
in green spandex tights
with a big fluffy hat
with a little star on the end,
crouched over a desk
with pencil in hand,
grumbling to himself about
how much he hates his job,
ask him carefully
what the differences are between
a fluffy duck-shaped cloud
swimming lazily in the sky
and a duck lazily swimming in a lake
with its head in the clouds.

Or, if the above directions seem like
too much work,
just sit in a park and pick some dandelions.
