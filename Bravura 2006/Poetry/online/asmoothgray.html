A Smooth, Gray Rock
By Linda Roux


“Read me New Hampshire’s record catches.”

        “Landlocked salmon,” she reads. “Thirty-six inches.
        Eighteen pounds, eight ounces, 1914.”

“Go on.” What a sight that must have been.
A bit bigger than my catch in ‘49.

        “Lake trout, thirty-nine and one half inches.
        Twenty-eight pounds and eight ounces, caught by
        Albert C. Staples of Massachusetts
        on April 24, 1958.”

In a stream as clean as a Quaker prayer
salmon swim against the ice-cold current
as if trying to hook their downcurved snouts
on backward-running, rushing water.

        “Black crappie, seventeen and one quarter inches.
        Tom Noyes of New Hampshire, February 2000.”

My daughter is 40 but doesn’t know how to cry.
She’s impacted like these bowels of mine.
My kidneys, my heart, all are shutting down.
So I watch the coho or trout or sockeye
swimming across this tiresome ceiling.
“Thank you, sweetheart. I’ll rest now.”

Waking, I wonder where she’s gone.
Now I’m no longer a member of
the tribe who walk when and where they want.
I hear doors open and close several times.
After a while she walks into my room
and places something from Alaska in my hand.
She must have found it among the seashells
on the kitchen windowsill,
a water-smoothed rock, but grainy, and shaped
as if made to fit in my rough, cupped hand.
She had put it outside to warm in the day
then she brought it in, the last sunlight,
so I could feel it on my hands again.
