Still Water
By Brent Ohlendorf


It took me a while to see what had died,
yet even now I still treat it as living.
You do the same I assume, making sure to comfort it’s corpse.
We both speak of the deceased as living, as breathing.
And as breath becomes whispers, whispers become words,
the lingering candor of a broken being
becomes little more than a bewildered memory.
It’s life, a lie no doubt, your lie reflecting mine, mine reflecting yours,
like a mirror over still water,
the perceived infinity only an illusion in the reflection,
only a dream mistakenly seen as life.

I used to blame fate for devotion’s departure, always pitting us against one another.
Then I blamed myself, easily a scapegoat.
Then came God, society, time and space.
I blamed anything and everything.
Then I stopped blaming all together.
I realized that if fate planned passion’s demise, it did its job.
If God and society really wanted a casualty, then there’s no arguing the Will.
Time and space were always in the realms of science,
and I hope affection isn’t easily explained away as a chemical imbalance.
That last anything and everything, well…that used to be you,
and I’m done blaming you.

Yet without anyone to blame, I’m left with nothing.
No tears for the departed, even though I wish there were.
I’ve seen the desert rose in a summer’s rain,
so much beauty destroyed by thirst,
life not seen as precious until it no longer exists.
Yet my indifference is worn as an emperor’s clothing,
the nothingness oozing out as an almost endless apathy towards you.
I know now tears of apathy don’t exist.
Tears of a lost life are useless.

‘In Memoriam’ provides little solace
as I assure you Tennyson was wrong in his assumption.
And believe that Severin taught me nothing,
for you needed no whip or furs my Venus,
only a life to forget and a death not to be mourned,
a dreamless sleep not to remember.
Yet now I see dreams don’t end, you merely wake up,
and it’s the reality that I will never again wake up next to you,
that keeps me asleep,
that keeps my lie below yours,
In the still water I should have known never to drink.
